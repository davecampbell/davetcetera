This Is I.T.

Approaches and suggestions for defining Information Technology

The Problem with "Computer Technology"

Restricting IT to computers is a common but historically shallow framing. It ignores the decades of foundational work that made computing possible — and the broader truth that information, not hardware, is the subject. The better question is: what does IT do? It captures, processes, stores, transmits, and retrieves information. The machinery changes; the mission doesn't.

Approaches to Defining I.T.

1. The Functional Approach

Define IT by what it does, not what it is made of:

Any technology whose primary purpose is to handle information — acquiring, storing, processing, transmitting, or displaying it.

This immediately includes:

  • Telegraphs and telephone switches
  • Radio and television broadcasting infrastructure
  • Punch card systems (pre-electronic)
  • Modern computing, networking, and AI

Why this works: It's technology-agnostic and future-proof. When something new arrives (quantum computing, biological computing), you can ask "does it handle information?" rather than "does it look like a computer?"

2. The Historical / Lineage Approach

Trace IT back to its intellectual origins rather than its commercial ones.

Key waypoints:

  • 1840s — Telegraph: the first system to separate information from a physical carrier (the message from the messenger)
  • 1876 — Telephone: real-time voice as data
  • Bell Labs (1920s–1970s) — Transistor, information theory, Shannon's entropy, error correction, signal processing. This is arguably the true birthplace of modern IT
  • 1948 — Claude Shannon's A Mathematical Theory of Communication — the foundational paper that defines information mathematically and underpins all digital systems
  • 1950s–60s — Mainframe computing emerges from this lineage, not apart from it
  • 1970s–present — Personal computing, internet, mobile, cloud

The takeaway: computers didn't create IT; IT created computers. The field existed before the transistor.

3. The Information Science Approach

Information technology is a subset of the broader field of information science, which includes:

  • How information is structured and classified (library science, ontologies, metadata)
  • How it is communicated (linguistics, semiotics, media studies)
  • How it is secured and controlled (cryptography, policy)
  • How it is used by humans (HCI, cognitive science)

This approach connects IT to disciplines that don't typically appear in a CS curriculum but are inseparable from it in practice. Cybersecurity, UX, data governance, and AI ethics all live here.

4. The Sociotechnical Approach

IT doesn't exist in isolation — it's always embedded in social systems. Defining it purely as hardware or software misses the human layer.

IT is the ensemble of tools, practices, standards, and institutions that societies use to manage information.

This includes:

  • Postal systems (organised information routing)
  • Filing and records management
  • Standards bodies (IEEE, IETF, ISO) — the "invisible infrastructure"
  • Data centres as physical/social infrastructure

Why this is useful for teaching: It prevents students from thinking of IT as purely technical, which helps when addressing ethics, policy, and real-world impact.

5. The Shannon / Entropy Definition

For a more rigorous framing: information, in the technical sense, is reduction of uncertainty. IT is any system that does this systematically.

A traffic light is a rudimentary IT system. A spreadsheet is an IT system. So is a filing cabinet. What makes modern IT distinctive is:

  • Scale (billions of operations, billions of users)
  • Speed (real-time or near-real-time)
  • Programmability (the system can be reconfigured without physical change)

This framing lets you distinguish IT from other technologies while explaining why electronics and software became central — they maximise all three properties.

Suggested Scope for a Working Definition

Rather than one rigid definition, consider a layered scope:

Layer Includes Era
Core Computing, networking, software, data storage 1950s–present
Historical roots Telegraphy, telephony, radio, information theory 1840s–1940s
Adjacent disciplines Information science, cognitive science, media Ongoing
Emerging AI, quantum, biocomputing, spatial computing Now and forward

The "core" is what most people mean by IT in everyday use. The outer layers are what separate a practitioner from a technician.

Recommendations for the Course / Context

  1. Open with Bell Labs — it's one of the most productive research environments in history and draws a direct line from physics → information theory → computing → the internet. It immediately challenges the "IT = computers" assumption.

  2. Use Shannon's definition of information as an early anchor. It's rigorous, non-circular, and gives students a durable mental model that survives technological change.

  3. Include one non-digital case study (e.g., the London postal system in the 1800s, or telephone exchange operators) to demonstrate that information systems predate electronics.

  4. Frame computers as an instantiation of IT, not its definition — the way an internal combustion engine is one instantiation of the idea of a heat engine.

  5. Acknowledge the fuzzy boundary — IT overlaps with electrical engineering, communications, media, and management. That's not a weakness; it's evidence that information is genuinely central to modern life.
