Discussion 01 — Approaching the ITHS Curation Ontology

Purpose of This Document

A working discussion space for figuring out how the ITHS Curation Committee should approach:

  1. Defining the structure of an ontology for IT historical artifacts, people, and relationships
  2. Translating that ontology into a database architecture
  3. Running the process collaboratively as a group (not just technically)

Part 1 — The Core Problem

An ontology for a historical society isn't just a schema. It's a set of claims about what kinds of things exist, how they relate, and what the organization believes is worth tracking. Before touching any database tool, the committee needs to answer questions like:

  • What is the primary unit of record? (An artifact? A product line? A company? A person? An event?)
  • What does "historical significance" mean for this organization? (Who decides? By what criteria?)
  • How do we model influence and lineage? ("X led to Y" is a relationship, but there are many kinds of causality)
  • What is the boundary between canonical record and user contribution?

These are editorial and philosophical questions, not technical ones. The database follows from the answers.

Part 2 — How Other Groups Have Done This

These examples are chosen because they faced the same core challenge: a community needed to agree on what things are before they could build anything.

2.1 — The Getty Vocabularies (Art & Architecture)

The Getty Research Institute maintains three controlled vocabularies — the Art & Architecture Thesaurus (AAT), the Union List of Artist Names (ULAN), and the Thesaurus of Geographic Names (TGN). These are among the most mature cultural heritage ontologies in existence.

Relevance to ITHS:

  • They separate concept records (what a thing is, abstractly) from instance records (a specific object or person)
  • ULAN explicitly models relationships between artists: teacher/student, collaborator, influenced-by
  • The AAT has a formal process for community submissions and editorial review — this maps well to the "canonical vs. user submission" problem

Key lesson: They started with use cases, not with schema. "What questions do curators actually ask?" drove the structure.

Reference: getty.edu/research/tools/vocabularies

2.2 — Wikidata

Wikidata is a general-purpose, community-maintained knowledge graph that underlies much of Wikipedia's structured data. Everything is a statement about an item, with properties and qualifiers.

Relevance to ITHS:

  • It explicitly handles the "X influenced Y" problem via properties like influenced by, has part, follows, followed by
  • It has a clean separation between the item (canonical) and references (sourced claims)
  • It's entirely open and the data model documentation is excellent for learning how to think about these problems

Key lesson: Their data model evolved through community consensus via "WikiProjects" — groups that decided on the properties and structure for a domain (e.g., WikiProject Video Games, WikiProject Computing). This is a direct model for how the ITHS committee could work.

Reference: wikidata.org/wiki/Wikidata:WikiProjects

2.3 — The Computer History Museum (CHM) — Collection Catalog

The CHM has an online collection catalog that models hardware, software, people, and corporate history. They've had to solve the same "what is a canonical item?" question — is the canonical item the product design, the specific manufactured unit, or the product line?

Relevance to ITHS:

  • Their distinction between artifact (a physical thing), object type (a class of thing), and lot (a collection) maps onto the ITHS problem of personal submissions vs. canonical records
  • They use a controlled vocabulary for significance/impact categories

Key lesson: They distinguish "what the object is" from "what the object means" — physical description is separated from historical narrative. This prevents the ontology from collapsing under the weight of interpretation.

Reference: computerhistory.org/collections/

2.4 — The Prelinger Archives / Internet Archive — Metadata Ontologies

Rick Prelinger's film archive and the broader Internet Archive have grappled with how to describe media items that have complex provenance chains — one film might be a copy of a copy, with annotations added by multiple hands.

Relevance to ITHS:

  • The "user submits their copy or story of a canonical item" feature is essentially a provenance and versioning problem
  • Dublin Core and Schema.org (which IA uses heavily) are good prior art for simple, flat metadata that is still interoperable

Key lesson: Don't invent what already exists. Dublin Core, Schema.org, and SKOS cover a huge amount of the basic metadata and relationship vocabulary. Start by mapping your concepts to these before creating custom properties.

2.5 — Open Library (Internet Archive) — Works, Editions, and Copies

Open Library models the same three-tier structure that ITHS likely needs:

Open Library ITHS Equivalent
Work (abstract book) Canonical item (e.g., "the Apple II")
Edition (specific publication) Product variant (e.g., "Apple II Plus, 1979")
Copy (a user's physical book) User submission ("my Apple II, serial #...")

Key lesson: This hierarchy is well-understood and worth adopting directly. It cleanly separates the abstract thing from its instantiations.

Reference: openlibrary.org/developers/api

Part 3 — A Proposed Process for ITHS

Based on the above examples, here is a rough sequence for how the committee might work:

Phase 1 — Use Case Collection (non-technical)

Before any data modeling, collect 10–15 concrete questions that the database should be able to answer. Examples:

  • "What hardware innovations came out of Xerox PARC and what products did they influence?"
  • "Show me a timeline of storage media from punch cards to SSDs"
  • "Who were the key engineers at DEC and what did they go on to build?"
  • "What members have submitted photos of Apple I machines?"

These become the test cases for the ontology. If a proposed schema can't answer them, it's incomplete.

Phase 2 — Entity and Relationship Brainstorm

Working from the use cases, list the types of things and types of relationships that appear. Don't worry about the database yet. A whiteboard or a simple document is fine.

Entity types might include: Person, Organization, Product, ProductLine, Concept/Innovation, Event, Document/Publication, PhysicalArtifact (user submission).

Relationship types might include: EmployedBy, InventedBy, InfluencedBy, PrecededBy, PartOf, VersionOf, ContributedTo.

Phase 3 — Map to Existing Vocabularies

Before designing custom schema, check if Schema.org, Wikidata properties, or the Getty AAT already have the concept. This saves time and enables future interoperability.

Phase 4 — Draft Ontology in Plain Language

Write entity definitions and relationship definitions in plain sentences before touching any technical notation. Example:

A Product is a commercially released item (hardware or software) with a known manufacturer and release date. A Product may have a PrecededBy relationship to another Product if the earlier Product was a direct technical or commercial predecessor.

Phase 5 — Prototype with a Small Slice

Pick one domain (e.g., personal computers from 1975–1985) and build the schema for just that slice. Load a small amount of real data. See what breaks.

Phase 6 — Technical Architecture Decision

Only after Phases 1–5 is it worth deciding: relational, graph, or hybrid? Document store? The answer should fall out of the data naturally, not be chosen up front.

Open Questions for the Committee

  1. What is the minimum viable set of entity types to launch with?
  2. Who has editorial authority over canonical records vs. user submissions?
  3. Is the goal a public-facing database, an internal curatorial tool, or both?
  4. What existing databases (Wikidata, CHM catalog) should we link to rather than duplicate?
  5. How do we handle contested history? (Competing claims of "who invented X")

Next Steps
